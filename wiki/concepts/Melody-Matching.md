# Melody-Matching

Julian Bradley's technique for choosing which chord voicing to play when reading a lead sheet. The core idea: pick a voicing whose **top note is the melody note**. This way, playing the chord and playing the melody become one action.

---

## The three steps

1. **Read the chord symbol** → identify the chord type (maj7, min7, V7, etc.)
2. **Read the melody note** → identify its scale degree relative to the chord (is it the 3rd? the 9th? the 7th?)
3. **Choose a voicing** for that chord type that has the melody note as its top note

---

## Quick reference table

| Melody degree | Good voicing choice | Top note |
|---------------|--------------------|----|
| 3rd | [[Shell Voicings]] position A (1 7 3) or [[Open Voicings]] 1 5 7 **3** | 3rd |
| 5th | [[Open Voicings]] 1 7 3 **5** or [[Rootless Voicings]] position B (7 9 3 **5**) | 5th |
| 7th | [[Shell Voicings]] position B (1 3 7) or [[Open Voicings]] 1 5 3 **7** | 7th |
| 9th | [[Rootless Voicings]] position A (3 5 7 **9**) or [[Fourth Voicings]] sawn-off (1 7 / 3 6 **9**) | 9th |
| 11th | [[Kenny Barron Voicing]] for min7 (1 5 9 / 3 7 **11**) | 11th |
| #11th | [[Kenny Barron Voicing]] for maj7 (1 5 9 / 3 7 **#11**) | #11th |

## Practical note

You only need to look at the **first melody note for each chord**. Most melodies move after the chord has sounded — hold the voicing down and let the melody continue above (or release the top notes if the melody dips back into the voicing range).

As you play through a standard this way, you'll naturally use a mix of voicing types — shells, open, rootless, 4ths — which creates variety and interest in the arrangement.

---

## Example: 'Misty' (first bars)

| Chord | Melody note | Degree | Voicing used |
|-------|-------------|--------|--------------|
| Ebmaj7 | Bb | 5th | Open voicing (1 7 3 5) |
| Bbm7 | Ab | 7th | Shell position B (1 3 7) |
| Eb7 | Bb | 5th | Rootless position B |
| Abmaj7 | C | 3rd | Open voicing (1 5 7 3) |

---

## Why it works

The chord voicing already sounds good on its own. By matching the melody note to the top of the voicing, you're not adding an untested note on top — the melody note is already proven to sound good as part of the voicing.
