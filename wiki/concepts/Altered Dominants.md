# Altered Dominants

Dominant 7th chords (V7) can carry **altered extensions** — notes that are deliberately raised or lowered from the major scale. These create extra tension that wants to resolve into the I chord. All altered extensions live exclusively on V7 chords.

---

## The altered extensions

| Extension | Symbol | Notes on C7 | Character |
|-----------|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Flat 9 | C7(b9) | + Db | Dark, menacing tension |
| Sharp 9 | C7(#9) | + D#/Eb | Bluesy, aggressive ("James Bond Chord") |
| Sharp 11 | C7(#11) | + F# | Lydian dominant flavor, bright tension |
| Flat 13 | C7(b13) | + Ab | Implies altered scale — see below |

Multiple alterations can stack: C7(#11/b9), C7(b9/b13), etc.

---

## C7 altered

When you see **C7(b13)** or just "C7alt", it implies the full **C altered scale** — also called the Lydian Dominant b2 scale or the 7th mode of Db melodic minor:

**C altered scale:** C – Db – Eb – E – Gb – Ab – Bb

This scale contains all possible alterations simultaneously (b9, #9, #11, b13), so C7b13 is shorthand for "play everything altered."

---

## The G altered scale — practical mnemonic

To play the altered scale on any dominant, **use the melodic minor scale starting one half-step above**:

> G altered = A♭ melodic minor (start on A♭: A♭ – B♭ – B – D♭ – E♭ – F – G)

This works for any dominant chord: V7alt → start melodic minor on the note a half-step above the root.

---

## Where altered dominants appear

- On the **V chord** before resolution to a I chord (especially in minor ii-V-I)
- As **tritone substitutions** — see [[Tritone Substitution]] for how the two strategies connect
- At **phrase endings** where extra harmonic tension is desired

### Minor ii-V-I example (in C minor):
```
ii:  Dø        (D half-diminished)
V:   G7(b9)    (altered dominant)
i:   Cm        (resolution)
```
