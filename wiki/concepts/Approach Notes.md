# Approach Notes

A technique for making improvised lines more fluid and jazz-idiomatic by approaching **target notes** (chord tones) from adjacent pitches rather than landing on them directly.

## What is a target note?

Any chord tone you want to emphasize — the root, 3rd, 5th, or 7th of the current chord. Approach notes are the notes that lead *into* the target.

---

## Type 1 — Half-step below (chromatic lower neighbor)

Play the note **one half-step below** the target, then step up.

```
Target: C  →  approach from: B
Target: E  →  approach from: E♭
Target: G  →  approach from: F♯
```

This is the most common approach in jazz. It sounds pulled and inevitable — the leading-tone effect applied to any chord tone.

**Piano application:** When starting a phrase on a chord tone, first play the half-step below on the beat before, then land on the target on the strong beat.

---

## Type 2 — Chromatic enclosure

Surround the target from **both sides** — approach from above *and* below — before landing.

### Pattern: above → below → target
```
Target: C  →  D♭ → B → C
Target: E  →  F  → E♭ → E
```

### Pattern: below → above → target
```
Target: C  →  B → D♭ → C
```

Enclosures typically take 2–3 notes to resolve. They create a brief suspension before the chord tone and are highly characteristic of bebop.

**Piano application:** Use enclosures at the start of a phrase or when entering a new chord. Works especially well on the 3rd or 7th of a chord.

---

## Type 3 — Scale-step above (diatonic upper neighbor)

Play the note **one scale step above** the target, then step down. Less spicy than chromatic approaches but smooth and lyrical.

---

## In practice: target the guide tones

The most impactful targets are the **3rd and 7th** of each chord (the guide tones). Approaching these defines the harmony without needing the root.

| Chord | Strong targets | Half-step below approach |
|-------|---------------|--------------------------|
| Dm7   | F (3rd), C (7th) | E → F, B → C          |
| G7    | B (3rd), F (7th) | B♭ → B, E → F          |
| Cmaj7 | E (3rd), B (7th) | E♭ → E, B♭ → B         |

---

## Combining approaches in a lick

A typical bebop phrase:
1. Start with an enclosure of the 3rd
2. Run scale tones through the chord
3. End with a half-step approach into the 7th of the next chord
