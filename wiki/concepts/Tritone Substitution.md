# Tritone Substitution

A reharmonization technique where any **dominant 7th chord** is replaced by the dominant 7th chord whose root lies a **tritone away** (6 half-steps).

## The rule

> Replace V7 with the dominant chord a tritone away from it.

| Original | Tritone sub |
|----------|-------------|
| G7       | D♭7         |
| C7       | F♯7         |
| F7       | B7          |
| B♭7      | E7          |

## Why it works

Both chords share the same **tritone interval** — the two notes that create the dominant chord's tension:

- G7 contains B (major 3rd) and F (♭7) → tritone B–F
- D♭7 contains F (major 3rd) and C♭/B (♭7) → tritone F–C♭ = F–B

Same tritone, different root. The ear still hears the dominant tension resolving to the tonic.

## Voice leading advantage

The tritone sub root (D♭) is a half-step above the tonic (C), creating smooth **chromatic bass motion**:
```
… D♭7 → Cmaj7
bass:  D♭  →  C   (half-step down)
```
Compared to a perfect-fourth drop (G→C), the half-step resolution is much smoother and more modern-sounding.

## In a ii–V–I

```
Standard:  Dm7 – G7  – Cmaj7
With sub:  Dm7 – D♭7 – Cmaj7
```
The bass line becomes D → D♭ → C — a chromatic descent.

## Application at the piano

- Use the tritone sub in place of V7 at phrase endings for a more sophisticated sound.
- In the right hand, the chord voicing barely changes — the 3rd and 7th of G7 (B, F) become the 7th and 3rd of D♭7.
- The rootless voicing of G7 (B–F–A or A–D–F–B depending on position) is **identical** to the rootless voicing of D♭7 with inverted roles.

## Relationship to altered dominants

The tritone sub is closely related to [[Altered Dominants]]: the altered scale of G (G altered = A♭ melodic minor) contains D♭ as its ♭5, connecting the two substitution strategies.
