# Open Voicings

Four-note voicings built from the chord tones (1 3 5 7), but with the notes **spread wide** across bass, mid, and treble instead of stacked in order. Requires two hands. Sounds clean and spacious compared to [[Shell Voicings]] (which sit within less than an octave).

---

## The three open voicings (demonstrated on C major 7)

Choose which one to use based on the **melody note** — use the voicing that has the melody note as its top note:

| Melody note | Voicing (bottom → top) | Notes in C |
|-------------|------------------------|------------|
| 7th | 1 5 3 **7** | C G E B |
| 3rd | 1 5 7 **3** | C G B E |
| 5th | 1 7 3 **5** | C B E G |

---

## Adapting to other chord types

Adjust the 3rd and 7th to match the chord type — the structure stays the same:

| Chord | Voicing A (top = 7th) | Voicing B (top = 3rd) | Voicing C (top = 5th) |
|-------|----------------------|-----------------------|-----------------------|
| C major 7 | C G E B | C G B E | C B E G |
| C minor 7 | C G Eb Bb | C G Bb Eb | C Bb Eb G |
| C7 | C G E Bb | C G Bb E | C Bb E G |
| Cø (half-dim) | C Gb Eb Bb | C Gb Bb Eb | C Bb Eb Gb |

---

## Suggested use

Open voicings work best when:
- The melody note is a **chord tone** (1, 3, 5, or 7)
- You want a **clean, resonant sound** with some bass weight
- Contrast against shell voicings in an arrangement (shells = minimal, open = full)

They can be transposed up or down an octave — use your ear to decide which register sounds best.

---

## In the voicing system

Open voicings sit between shells and rootless in complexity. For the full picture see [[Chord Voicing Principles]]. For choosing between voicings live, see [[Melody-Matching]].
