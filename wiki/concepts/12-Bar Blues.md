# 12-Bar Blues

The most important musical form in blues and a cornerstone of jazz. Twelve bars structured around three chords.

---

## The form

```
Bars  1–4:  | I7  | I7  | I7  | I7  |
Bars  5–6:  | IV7 | IV7 |
Bars  7–8:  | I7  | I7  |
Bars  9–10: | V7  | IV7 |
Bars 11–12: | I7  | V7  |  ← turnaround
```

All three chords are **dominant 7th chords** (e.g., in C: C7, F7, G7) — this is what distinguishes the blues from standard major/minor harmony.

### In C
```
| C7 | C7 | C7 | C7 |
| F7 | F7 | C7 | C7 |
| G7 | F7 | C7 | G7 |
```

The turnaround (bar 12: V7) pulls the listener back to bar 1.

---

## The 10 left-hand patterns

### Pattern 1 — Root only
Whole notes on the root of each chord. Bare and open.

### Pattern 2 — Root + 5th
Play root and 5th together. Gives bass stability without rhythm.

### Pattern 3 — Root–5th–6th cell (swing)
```
I → 5th → 6th (swing eighths)
e.g., C → G → A
```
Classic blues/boogie swing feel.

### Pattern 4 — Root–5th–♭7–octave
Dominant 7th feel in the bass line itself.

### Pattern 5 — Boogie-woogie bass
Root on beat 1, alternating between 5th and 6th on the offbeats. Creates the driving boogie shuffle feel.

### Pattern 6 — Walking bass
Single quarter notes connecting chord tones using the pattern **I → III → V → VI → [next root]**. The chromatic passing note (♭III → III) is a key ingredient.

```
C blues bar 1 (C7):  C – E – G – A
                     moving toward F on beat 1 of bar 5
```

### Pattern 7 — Chromatic approach (♭III → III)
Use E♭ → E in the bass as a pickup into the chord tone E. The quintessential blues bass lick.

### Pattern 8 — Shuffle pattern
Triplet-feel variation: long–short rhythmic cell repeated. The canonical blues shuffle.

### Pattern 9 — Broken octave bass
Alternating between root in bass register and root one octave up. Creates rhythmic bounce.

### Pattern 10 — Full chord comping
Left hand plays the full chord (I7, IV7, V7) with comping rhythm. Allows right hand to play melody or improvise freely.

---

## Walking bass in depth

Walking bass moves in **quarter notes**, one note per beat. The goal is to:
1. Land on the **root** of the new chord on beat 1
2. Use **chord tones** (I, III, V, VI) as primary notes
3. Fill gaps with **chromatic approach notes** (one half-step below the destination)

| Bar | Chord | Walk |
|-----|-------|------|
| 1   | C7    | C – E – G – A |
| 2   | C7    | B♭ – G – E – D♭ (approaching F) |
| 5   | F7    | F – A – C – D |

---

## Applying at the piano

- Start with Pattern 3 (root–5th–6th) to get the blues feel in the left hand.
- Once comfortable, add walking bass in Pattern 6.
- Right hand can play [[Blues Scale]] lines or melody while LH walks.
- Practice slowly with a metronome; the swing feel comes from note duration, not speed.
