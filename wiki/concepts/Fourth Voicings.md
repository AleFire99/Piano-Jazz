# Fourth Voicings

Voicings built in **4ths** rather than 3rds. Traditional chords stack 3rds (C E G B D). Fourth voicings rearrange the same notes into a series of 4ths (B E A D G C), giving a more modern, open, quartal sound.

---

## Structure

Start from the chord's **7th** and build upward in 4ths using notes from the scale. Three notes in each hand:

- **Left hand:** 7 – 3 – 6 (three 4ths up from the 7th)
- **Right hand:** 9 – 5 – 1 (continuing three more 4ths up)
- Full: **(7 3 6 / 9 5 1)**

### C major 7 example:
- LH: B – E – A (7 – 3 – 6)
- RH: D – G – C (9 – 5 – 1)

### C minor 7 (flatten 3rd and 7th, all other notes stay):
- LH: Bb – Eb – A (7 – 3 – 6)
- RH: D – G – C (9 – 5 – 1)

### C7 (flatten 3rd and 7th to fit dominant):
- LH: Bb – E – A (7 – 3 – 6)
- RH: D – G – C (9 – 5 – 1)

---

## Suggested use

**With a band (or bass player):** Play the full two-handed version. The root can be left to the bassist.

**Solo piano (sawn-off version):** Drop the top two notes from the right hand and restructure:
- LH: 1 – 7 (root and 7th)
- RH: 3 – 6 – 9 (three upper color notes)
- Full: **(1 7 / 3 6 9)**

Example for C major 7 solo: LH = C B, RH = E A D. The 9th (D) is the melody note on top — ideal when melody is the **9th**.

---

## Sound character

Fourth voicings have a distinctly **modern, open** quality. They sound less "lush" than rootless voicings and less grounded than open voicings. Common in post-bop and contemporary jazz piano.
