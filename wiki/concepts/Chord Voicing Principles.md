# Chord Voicing Principles

Three principles that apply to all professional jazz piano chord voicings. These are strong guidelines, not rigid rules — exceptions exist.

---

## 1. Play each note once (no repeated notes)

Each finger contributes a new tonal color. If your left hand already has the 3rd, don't play the 3rd again in your right hand — use that finger to add a new color (9th, 11th, 13th, etc.).

## 2. Root, 3rd, and 7th voiced at the bottom

These three notes define the chord type (maj7, min7, or V7). They carry more weight in the bass register, so they belong at the bottom of the voicing. Once they're established, extensions (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) are added higher up where they add color without confusing the listener.

## 3. Leave space for the melody

The melody needs to be heard clearly above the chord. Leave a gap of at least a **minor 3rd** between the melody note (the highest note you're playing) and the note directly below it (which is part of the chord voicing). Without this gap, the chord sounds muddy and the melody gets lost.

---

## The five voicing types

These principles apply to all five types of jazz chord voicing:

| Voicing | Notes | Hands | Best use |
|---------|-------|-------|----------|
| [[Shell Voicings]] | 1 3 7 | One | Bare/minimal sound |
| [[Open Voicings]] | 1 5 3 7 (spread) | Two | Clean, with bass |
| [[Rootless Voicings]] | 3 5 7 9 | Two | Colorful, most versatile |
| [[Kenny Barron Voicing]] | 1 5 9 / 3 7 11 | Two | When melody is 11th/#11th |
| [[Fourth Voicings]] | 7 3 6 / 9 5 1 | Two | With band; modern sound |

## Practice approach

- Learn one voicing type at a time — spend a week on each before moving to the next
- Use **side-slipping**: start from C, then transpose the voicing up/down by various intervals until you can play it from all 12 notes
- **Count interval patterns**, not note names (e.g., "root, up a major 3rd, up a minor 3rd" — not "C E G")
- See [[Melody-Matching]] for how to choose which voicing to use when playing a lead sheet
