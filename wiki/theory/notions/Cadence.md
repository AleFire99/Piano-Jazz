
A **cadence** is a progression of at least two chords that signals the end of a musical phrase, section, or piece. It creates a sense of resolution or continuation. Understanding cadences is essential for interpreting musical structure and harmony.

There are two main types of cadences: **finished** and **unfinished**. Finished cadences bring a sense of closure, while unfinished cadences leave the music open-ended, creating a feeling of anticipation.

---

## Finished Cadences
Finished cadences resolve the harmonic tension and end on chord **I** (the tonic chord), providing a sense of stability and closure.

### 1. Perfect (Authentic) Cadence
- **Progression**: **V – I**
- **Description**: This is the most conclusive cadence, providing a strong sense of finality. It is often used at the end of a piece or section, marking a full resolution.

### 2. Plagal Cadence ("Amen" Cadence)
- **Progression**: **IV – I**
- **Description**: This cadence is commonly referred to as the "Amen" cadence due to its frequent use at the end of hymns. It has a softer resolution compared to the authentic cadence.

---

## Unfinished Cadences
Unfinished cadences do **not** resolve to chord **I**, leaving the phrase open-ended and creating a sense of tension or anticipation.

### 1. Imperfect (Half) Cadence
- **Progression**: **I/II/IV – V**
- **Description**: This cadence ends on **V**, leaving the phrase unresolved and creating an expectation of continuation. It is commonly used as a lead-in to the next phrase or section.

### 2. Interrupted (Deceptive) Cadence
- **Progression**: **V – VI**
- **Description**: This cadence creates the expectation of a perfect cadence (**V – I**) but surprises the listener by resolving to the **VI** chord instead. It interrupts the expected resolution, creating a deceptive ending.

---

## How Cadences Relate to Scales
Cadences play a crucial role in harmonic structure and can guide your scale practice. Knowing how different cadences resolve within a key can help you navigate chord progressions and improve your ability to anticipate harmonic changes in your playing or composition.

By understanding these cadences, you'll gain a deeper insight into how musical phrases function and how you can use scales to improvise and build harmonic progressions.
