# Pillar — Voicings

Voicings are how you arrange the notes of a chord on the piano. The same chord can sound sparse or lush, open or tight, simple or complex — all depending on voicing. Mastering voicings is what separates generic-sounding piano from a professional jazz sound.

---

## The five voicing types (beginner → advanced)

| # | Voicing | Notes | Hands | Character |
|---|---------|-------|-------|-----------|
| 1 | [[Shell Voicings]] | Root, 3rd, 7th | One | Bare, minimal |
| 2 | [[Open Voicings]] | 1 5 3 7 (spread) | Two | Clean, spacious, with bass |
| 3 | [[Rootless Voicings]] | 3 5 7 9 | Two | Colorful, sophisticated — most used |
| 4 | [[Kenny Barron Voicing]] | 1 5 9 / 3 7 11 | Two | Modern, stacked 5ths, requires stretch |
| 5 | [[Fourth Voicings]] | 7 3 6 / 9 5 1 | Two | Quartal, open, post-bop |

Learn them in order. Spend a week on each before moving to the next. See [[Chord Voicing Principles]] for the three universal principles all voicings follow.

---

## Choosing which voicing to use

Use [[Melody-Matching]]: look at the melody note, identify its scale degree, pick a voicing that has that note on top. This is how you play chord and melody together in one hand position.

---

## Voice leading

A good voicing sequence minimizes finger movement between chords. The 3rd and 7th of each chord (**guide tones**) tend to resolve smoothly to the 7th and 3rd of the next chord, especially in ii-V-I progressions. Shell voicings make this most obvious; rootless voicings extend the same principle with more color.
