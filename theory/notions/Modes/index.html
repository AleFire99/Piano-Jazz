    Modes

    Modes of the Major Scale

    The modes are seven different scales derived from the major scale, each starting from a different degree of the scale. They offer unique sounds and are foundational to understanding harmony and melody in Western music.

    Ionian (I) – Major Scale

    The Ionian mode is simply the major scale. It is the most commonly used scale in Western music.

    notes  1 - 2 - 3 4 - 5 - 6 - 7 1
chords M - m - m M - M - m - ° M

    Characteristics

    • It is the most stable and resolved of all the modes.
    • Often used to express a happy, bright sound.
    • The tonic (I) provides a strong resolution in cadences.

    Dorian (II) – Minor Scale with a Natural 6

    The Dorian mode is a minor scale with a raised 6th degree. It blends the characteristics of both major and minor scales.

    notes  1 - 2 3 - 4 - 5 - 6 7 - 1
chords m - m M - M - m - ° M - m

    Characteristics

    • The natural 6th gives it a less somber and more open sound compared to the Aeolian mode.
    • Commonly used in jazz, blues, and folk music.
    • Provides a minor, yet more optimistic feel.

    Phrygian (III) – Minor Scale with a Flatted 2nd

    The Phrygian mode is a minor scale with a flattened 2nd degree, giving it a distinct, exotic flavor.

    notes  1 2 - 3 - 4 - 5 6 - 7 - 1
chords m M - M - m - º M - m - m

    Characteristics

    • The flattened 2nd degree gives it a tense, almost Spanish or Middle Eastern sound.
    • Often used in flamenco and metal music.
    • It has a darker, more mysterious feel.

    Lydian (IV) – Major Scale with a Sharped 4th

    The Lydian mode is a major scale with a raised 4th degree, giving it a dreamy and bright sound.

    notes  1 - 2 - 3 - 4 5 - 6 - 7 1
chords M - M - m - º M - m - m M

    Characteristics

    • The raised 4th creates a floating, ethereal quality.
    • Often used in film scores and progressive rock.
    • It has an uplifting and bright sound compared to the Ionian mode.

    Mixolydian (V) – Major Scale with a Flatted 7th

    The Mixolydian mode is a major scale with a flattened 7th degree, making it sound slightly bluesy or rock-oriented.

    notes  1 - 2 - 3 4 - 5 - 6 7 - 1
chords M - m - º M - m - m M - M

    Characteristics

    • The lowered 7th gives it a bluesy or rock feel.
    • Often used in rock, blues, and country music.
    • The flattened 7th creates a dominant seventh chord that resolves naturally to the tonic.

    Aeolian (VI) – Natural Minor Scale

    The Aeolian mode is simply the natural minor scale. It has a darker and more somber sound than the Ionian or Dorian modes.

    notes  1 - 2 3 - 4 - 5 6 - 7 - 1
chords m - º M - m - m M - M - m

    Characteristics

    • This scale has a more melancholic or serious tone.
    • Commonly used in classical, rock, and heavy metal music.
    • It often serves as the basis for minor key pieces and creates a sense of tension that resolves to the tonic.

    Locrian (VII) – Minor Scale with Flatted 2nd, 5th, and 6th

    The Locrian mode is a highly unstable scale, often considered the most dissonant due to its diminished fifth.

    notes  1 2 - 3 - 4 5 - 6 - 7 - 1
chords º M - m - m M - M - m - m

    Characteristics

    • The flatted 2nd, 5th, and 6th create a very tense and unstable sound.
    • It is rarely used as a key center, but it can be used to create tension in a piece.
    • Often found in jazz and experimental music where dissonance is explored.

    Conclusion

    Each mode brings its own unique flavor and emotion to music. Whether you're writing a melody or harmony, understanding these modes gives you more tools to express different moods and atmospheres. The modes can be used as a foundation for improvisation, composition, or simply to experiment with new sounds.
